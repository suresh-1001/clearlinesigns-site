About 3D Lobby Signs

How 3D Dimensional Lobby Signs
Are Built — and What to Expect

A 3D lobby sign is a dimensional sign — individual letters, a logo shape, or a full brand mark fabricated with physical depth and mounted directly to the reception wall. The material, thickness, finish, and mounting method all affect how the sign looks in your specific space. This page covers how these signs are built, what materials are available, and what the fabrication and installation process actually looks like.

In Silicon Valley, lobby signage is often the only physical brand touchpoint a client experiences. Your website, your pitch deck, your product — all of it gets filtered through a screen. The lobby wall is real. Companies that invest in a well-executed 3D lobby sign consistently report that it changes how visitors perceive the business before a word is spoken.

We've installed 3D lobby signs for tech companies, medical practices, law firms, financial services, nonprofits, and corporate campuses across San Jose and Silicon Valley — from seed-stage startups fitting out their first office to Fortune 500 companies refreshing their headquarters look. Sign Options

Types of 3D Lobby Signs
We Fabricate & Install

Brushed Aluminum Letters

Flat-cut or fabricated aluminum letters with a brushed or anodized finish. The go-to for tech companies, law firms, and financial services — clean, permanent, and reads immediately as high-end. Available in natural aluminum, painted, or powder-coated finishes.

Halo-Lit (Reverse-Lit) Logo Signs

Metal letters mounted slightly off the wall with LED lighting behind — the glow washes around each letter creating a halo effect. Dramatic in any lighting condition, especially effective in darker reception areas or against feature wall materials like wood or stone.

Acrylic Standoff Signs

A printed or face-lit acrylic panel mounted on brushed aluminum standoffs, floating off the wall surface. Clean modern look, highly versatile for logos with color gradients or complex artwork that's difficult to reproduce in cut metal.

Layered Acrylic Letters

Multiple acrylic layers bonded together for added depth — face color on a contrasting backer, sometimes with a metallic or frosted layer between. Popular for logos that need color accuracy and dimensional presence without the cost of full metal fabrication.

Painted Foam (HDU) Letters

High-density urethane foam CNC-cut and finished with premium paint. Significantly lighter than metal — the practical choice for large-format letters above 18 inches where wall backing would be required for aluminum. Finishes to painted or metallic appearance.

Mixed-Material Logo Signs

Combining metal letters with an acrylic logo panel, or dimensional letters on a printed backer board — for brands whose logo includes both text and graphic elements that are difficult to reproduce in a single material. We design the fabrication approach to match your brand standards exactly.

Common Questions

3D Lobby Sign Questions,
Answered

How thick should dimensional letters be?

Most reception signage uses ½" to 1" thickness for acrylic and aluminum letters — enough to read as dimensional without looking bulky. Foam letters are typically 1" to 2" thick since the material is lightweight relative to its size. We'll recommend a depth based on your letter height and viewing distance.

Will dimensional letters damage drywall when removed?

Standard mounting uses studs with adhesive and/or mechanical fasteners set into drywall anchors or wall studs where possible. Removal typically leaves small holes that patch easily — we can advise on a low-impact mounting method if you're in a leased space and need to restore the wall at lease-end.

Can you match our exact brand colors?

Yes. We work from Pantone references or brand guideline files for painted finishes, and from CMYK/RGB specs for printed acrylic panels. We produce a physical color sample for sign-off before fabricating the full piece when color accuracy is critical.

Do halo-lit signs need an electrician?

Halo-lit signs need a nearby power source — typically a standard outlet behind the wall or above the ceiling. If power isn't already in place, we coordinate with an electrician for the run. This is the main reason halo-lit projects have a longer lead time than non-illuminated options.
